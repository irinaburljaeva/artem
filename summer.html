The Rise of "Micro-Credentials"

Employers across the world are quietly rewriting the rules of hiring. Where a university degree once served as the primary signal of a candidate's abilities, many companies — particularly in tech, design, and creative industries — have begun placing greater emphasis on short, focused qualifications known as micro-credentials.

These courses, which typically take weeks rather than years to complete, cover specific skills such as data analysis, UX design, or digital marketing. Proponents argue that they allow learners to adapt quickly to a fast-changing job market and that their practical focus makes graduates immediately productive. Critics, however, warn that without proper regulation, the market risks becoming flooded with low-quality certificates that offer little real value.

The debate is particularly relevant for Generation Z, many of whom are questioning whether spending three or four years — and a significant amount of money — on a traditional degree is still the most sensible path. For now, most experts agree on one thing: the answer is unlikely to be the same for everyone.
